# Why Wolves Don't Chirp

[00:00] This video is sponsored by Brilliant. Hi, this is Kate from MinuteEarth. Say you’re in the forest, and you hear the chirp of a tree frog and the howl of a wolf. The good news is that the wolf is probably a lot farther away from you than the frog - a howl can travel a long way. But not because wolves are louder; a gray wolf and a gray tree frog actually call at about the same volume. It’s because the howl sound and the chirp sound behave very differently from each other - and that has big implications for why some animals chirp and others howl in the first place.

[00:33] Both sounds - all sounds actually - are waves of pressure that eventually vibrate parts of your auditory system. In a chirp, the waves are short and compact, so there are many waves per second, whereas in a howl, the waves are long and spread out so there are far fewer waves per second. This characteristic - the wave’s frequency - determines what a sound...well...sounds like; high-frequency waves sound high-pitched, and low-frequency waves sound low-pitched. But a wave’s frequency can also determine whether it even reaches your ears in the first place.

[01:05] High-frequency waves simply aren’t good at travelling long distances. The first reason is that, as a wave waves, it releases energy as heat, and the more frequently a wave waves, the more energy it releases as it travels along, and therefore the sooner it runs out of energy. So, a chirp will fade out over a shorter distance than a howl of equal volume. But there’s also the matter of obstacles. Low-frequency waves tend to bend around objects - a tree, a building, a Bigfoot - whereas high-frequency waves are much more likely to reflect off them.

[01:36] It’s like ocean waves hitting the coast; long, rolling waves kind of flow around it, while short, choppy waves get interrupted. In the same way, the short, choppy wave of a high-frequency chirp is likely to bounce away before reaching your ears. That’s why animals that communicate from afar - say, social animals that roam long distances, like wolves and elephants, or solitary animals in search of far-flung mates, like koalas and humpback whales - use really low-frequency calls that don’t run out of energy quickly or bounce off of things along the way.

[02:06] Elephants’ rumbles - which are so low-frequency that humans can’t hear them - can reach herdmates 10 kilometers away. And, since sounds tend to travel even farther in water than in air, some whales’ low-frequency songs can travel more than a thousand kilometers. Of course, animals can and do use medium- and even high-frequency calls to communicate, but since these calls fade out more quickly, the critters have to be much closer together. But what high-frequency calls are really good for is when animals want their calls to bounce off of stuff.

[02:36] Bats and dolphins, for instance, use high-frequency vocalizations precisely because these calls reflect off the tiniest of obstacles like an insect, a fish, or - in a bizarre experiment designed to test the limits of dolphin echolocation - a kernel of corn. Which...isn’t exactly something a dolphin would need. But in the end, communication - whether it’s high- or low-frequency - is all about making sure that your call is able to reach the right ear.
