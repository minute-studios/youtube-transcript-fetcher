# Which Planet Has The Strongest Quakes?

[00:00] When Earth's tectonic plates rub against one another, they create planet shaking shock waves that we Earthlings call earthquakes. Most other planetary bodies in our solar system don't have tectonic plates though. But somehow they do still have quakes. Hi, I'm Cameron and this is Minute Earth. Let's start with our neighbor Venus. It's home to lots of lava spewing volcanoes whose eruptions shake the planet's surface causing Earth or Venus quakes. Venus is so hot that it's more or less melted everything we've ever landed there. So, we've never actually measured a Venus quake, but scientists estimate that they're probably around magnitude 4 or greater on average, which is definitely enough to notice if you happen to be in the vicinity. Similarly, Pluto quakes might also be due to volcanoes, but there those volcanoes spew ice and ammonia rather than lava. So, it's a little bit different. Mars has gigantic dead volcanoes that suggest lots of big quakes in its distant past. But today, most Mars quakes probably happen because Mars core is cooling over time, shrinking the planet and causing the crust to crack and crumple. Of the 174 Mars quakes the Insight lander has detected so far, most wouldn't have been noticeable to a human standing on the surface. However, one, a magnitude 4.7, surely shook that little lander. Mercury likely has quakes for the same reason Mars does. One crack on Mercury is 1,000 km long with a ridge 3 km high, which suggests some violent Mercury quakes in the past. But because we've never landed anything on Mercury, we don't know how strong its quakes are today. The remaining planets, the gas giants, they lack a solid surface. So here, our definition of a quake gets a little more fluid. These planets weakly vibrate as fluids move around their rocky cores.

[01:47] The Cassini spacecraft was able to detect Saturn quakes by measuring vibrations in the planet's rings. But Jupiter quakes, Uranus quakes, and Neptune quakes still evade us. Of course, planets aren't the only celestial bodies in the solar system. Jupiter's moon Europa has icy quasi earthlike tectonic plates. And another of its moons, Io, has an estimated 400 active volcanoes that surely cause quakes. And we've been measuring quakes on our own moon since the 1960s. Thanks to the moon's slightly elliptical orbit, tidal forces shift its mass around deep below its crust, causing moon quakes, while temperature swings on the moon's surface also cause its crust to expand, contract, and yes, quake. Most moon quakes are pretty wimpy, but one, the strongest ever detected there, had a magnitude of 5.7, which would definitely knock the pictures off the wall of your moon base. But as you can see, none of the quakes we've measured in our solar system are anywhere nearly as strong as the record 9.6 magnitude earthquake off the coast of Chile in 1960. But even though there are so many objects in the solar system we don't know much about at the moment. Earth seems to be the queen of quakes. Wait a second. I forgot about the sun. Sunquakes happen when solar flares explode out of the sun disturbing its surface. The shock waves are so big that we can actually see them happening via specialized telescopes on satellites. In 1996, a sunquake created a 3 kilometer high shock wave with an estimated 11.3 magnitude. A magnitude that if it ever happened on Earth would rip our entire planet apart. How's that for an earthshattering revelation? You know what else can make the earth seem to move under your feet?

To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
- Cryovolcano:  a type of volcano that erupts gases and volatile material such as liquid water, ammonia, and hydrocarbons. 
- Helioseismology: the study of the Sun's interior structure and dynamics by analyzing the propagation of sound waves (or pressure waves) and oscillations on its surface.
- Ring Seismology: a field that studies the internal structure and dynamics of planets by analyzing the waves and patterns observed in their ring systems, essentially using the rings as a ""seismograph"" to probe the planet's interior. 
- Tectonic Activity: the movement and interaction of the Earth's lithospheric plates, leading to geological phenomena like earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, and mountain formation.
- Tidal Forces: the differential gravitational effects exerted by a celestial body (like the Moon or Sun) on different parts of a planet or object, causing it to stretch or bulge along the axis connecting the two bodies.
