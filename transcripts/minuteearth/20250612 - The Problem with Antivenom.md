# The Problem with Antivenom

[00:00] This video is sponsored by MyHeritage, more about them at the end of the video. Venomous snakes kill nearly 100,000 people a year. Which is weird, because we have a pretty effective treatment for venomous snake bites; it’s actually been around for more than a hundred years. So why are so many people still dying? Hi, I’m David and this is MinuteEarth. You probably know this treatment as “antivenom” or “antivenin.” Doctors inject proteins – specifically, antibodies – into the victim, which neutralize the snake’s venom… it’s basically a retroactive vaccination, and, when given in time, it can almost always save the victim’s life.

[00:33] But there’s a problem with antivenom… a few problems, actually. The first is actually making the stuff. Scientists haven’t yet been able to produce snake venom antibodies in a lab, so, instead, they rely on the immune systems of real live creatures to do it for them. First, they find – and milk! – a venomous snake. Then, they inject that venom into an animal – preferably something big, like a horse. But, because they don’t want to kill the horse, they dose it with the venom little by little, and wait for it to naturally produce antibodies. Finally, after collecting some of the horse’s blood and spinning it out, voila: antivenom.

[01:04] You can check out this video to see the whole process in action, but the point is that making even a single vial of antivenom is a really slow, expensive process – and snakebite victims often need several vials in order to recover. Then, there’s the problem that different snakes have different venom. See, over their 170-million-year history, venomous snakes have evolved venoms with different ways to target their prey; like, some venoms destroy nerves, some destroy muscles, and some destroy blood cells. Neutralizing each snake’s particular biochemical cocktail requires species-specific antibodies. And, actually, even snakes of the same species that live in different areas and eat different prey can have venoms that are different enough to require their own antibodies.

[01:41] So, in order to treat a snakebite victim, a doctor or clinic needs the right kind of antivenom. In some places, there’s only a few – or even just one – venomous snake species, so keeping the right antivenom on hand isn’t that big a deal. But in the tropics, where warm, stable climates foster lots of biodiversity, dozens of types of venomous snakes can coexist, requiring a whole collection of different antivenoms. And – for lots of reasons – communities in the tropics tend to be relatively poor, so clinics often don’t have the money to stock all these different – and expensive – antibodies. What’s more, since people in these places are more likely to work outside and less likely to wear protective footwear and clothing, they’re also at greater risk of snakebites in general.

[02:17] In other words, the people who are most likely to get bitten are the least likely to have access to the right treatment for those bites. That’s a big part of why venomous snakes kill 100,000 people every year and permanently disable another half a million… which really bites. Researchers are experimenting with ways to produce snake venom antibodies more quickly and cheaply, and they’re even working on a universal antivenom. But for now, decreasing the death toll of venomous snakes is going to require us to collectively agree that people shouldn’t have to risk death just because they were born in a place with more snakes and less money.

[02:47] And if we can do that, snakebite deaths could become a thing of the past. Or should we say, hissssstory.
