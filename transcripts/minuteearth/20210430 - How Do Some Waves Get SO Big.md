# How Do Some Waves Get SO Big?

[00:00] Hi, this is Kate from MinuteEarth. The biggest waves on Earth - those that are the height of entire office buildings - become truly gigantic because of what happens out of sight. That’s because waves don’t just sit on the surface; the waves’ energy can extend more than 1500 meters below it. Once a wave reaches shallow enough water, it makes contact with the seafloor. In most places around the world, the seafloor gently slopes upward, slowly pushing the wave’s energy above the surface, while friction against the seafloor slows it down.

[00:31] This creates an unstable column of water and energy that eventually comes crashing down; that’s a “break.” But the seafloor doesn't always slope gently, and its shape is what makes or breaks a break. Big underwater geological features like reefs can get in a wave's way and compress its energy. All that energy gets abruptly funneled up into a shallow channel - something like a four-lane highway’s worth of traffic being forced to suddenly merge into one lane.

[01:01] But while cars can use their brakes to slow down and dissipate their excess energy, waves can’t, and the resulting pile-up bursts over the barrier in huge, dramatic wave breaks. But the biggest waves on earth occur in places where undersea canyons not only funnel massive amounts of wave energy into a narrow channel, but also launch that energy over a shelf just before the shoreline. For example, during the most recent ice age, a combination of rivers and tectonic activity carved the V-shaped Nazaré Canyon into the continental shelf off Portugal's coast.

[01:35] And then oceans rose and filled it back in. As a wave moves through the 230-kilometer-long, ever-narrowing canyon, its energy gets funneled into a more focused - and therefore stronger - wave. But since the canyon remains several thousand meters deep, the wave energy inside it doesn’t touch the seafloor, and it doesn’t get slowed down...yet. Meanwhile, wave energy moving alongside the canyon’s edges gets bent inside it too - like multiple onramps adding cars to a highway - further boosting the wave’s energy.

[02:07] At the canyon’s narrowest point, right before the shore, the seafloor shoots up at a nearly vertical angle, creating a natural seawall that compresses all that energy - essentially blocking 7 lanes of an 8-lane highway. That energy then gets launched up; the resulting ten-story-tall breaks are truly the biggest waves you will ever "sea." LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
Amplitude - The distance between the crest and trough of a wave.
Bathymetry - The measurement of depth of water in oceans, seas, or lakes.
Nazaré Canyon - An undersea canyon just off the coast of Nazaré, Portugal, in the eastern North Atlantic Ocean. It is the largest submarine canyon in Europe, reaching depths of about 5,000 meters (16,000 ft) deep and a length of about 230 kilometers (140 mi).
Plunging Breaker - Occurs when there are rapid depth changes. The crest of the wave becomes very steep, and the rear of the wave violently plunges over the front. 
Reef Break - A location where waves break over a reef, often amplifying them. 
Spilling Breaker - Occurs when the seafloor slopes gradually. As the wave becomes unstable, energy is dissipated by water spilling over the crest of the wave. 
Swell - a slow, regular movement of the sea in rolling waves that do not break.
Wave period - The time between waves.
