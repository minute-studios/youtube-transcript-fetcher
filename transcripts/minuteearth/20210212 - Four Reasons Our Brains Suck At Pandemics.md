# Four Reasons Our Brains Suck At Pandemics

[00:00] Hi this is David from MinuteEarth in a special collaboration with Bill and Melinda Gates. The human brain is a frustrating thing. On the one hand, it has the analytical power to do the science to develop the tests and treatments and vaccines we need to stop COVID. On the other hand, it’s often irrational and makes us think and act in ways that help the virus spread. Here are four of those cognitive biases that have made the pandemic worse. First, we humans tend to underestimate a disease’s ability to spread. We intuitively process “growth” as linear: if one thing happens today, and one thing happens tomorrow, then we assume that another thing will happen the next day and - by the end of a month - 30 things will have happened.

[00:35] But infections like COVID can spread exponentially, so if one person is sick today and another gets sick tomorrow, the day after tomorrow four people could be sick, and by the end of the month, millions of us could be sick. The difference between this and this is HUGE - and it's the reason why many of us can't imagine - or can't believe - that we need to take such strict precautions. What’s more, our biased brains not only underestimate the risk of any one person getting sick - we also underestimate the chances that that one person is us! Ideally, in a pandemic, everyone should act pessimistically - like they're likely to get infected (or already are) - but that kind of thinking doesn’t come naturally to us.

[01:10] Humans are - by nature - optimists; Evidence shows that we think we're above average as far as intelligence, and less likely than average to get in a car accident. So it's no surprise that many people underestimate their likelihood of getting COVID - and act accordingly. The problem is, this overly optimistic behavior, when taken by lots of people, causes the virus to spread more quickly. We humans also have a bias against our future selves . We’re generally able to take stock of things and decide that temporary inconveniences like steering clear of each other outweigh temporary pleasures like - well - not steering clear of each other can help stop the pandemic sooner.

[01:43] But, in the moment, our brains’ desire for instant gratification means that we sometimes justify doing what we want - even if that screws over “future us”. And finally, we humans don’t like being told what not to do. When we hear - especially from an authority - “Don’t see your family” or “Don’t leave your house without a mask,” the primitive parts of our brains sense a threat. We get mad and become more likely to do the opposite…even when we know the guidelines will help limit the spread of disease! This effect is especially strong in places where “me” is valued over “we”. Even worse, once our hackles are raised, we’re more likely to buy into dangerous misinformation.

[02:17] Luckily, we humans have also identified some ways to combat our misguided instincts. Studies have found that regular reporting on the spread of the disease has helped people estimate risks and positive reinforcement from friends and family has helped people stay the course. So perhaps the rational parts of our brains could be the key to fighting the irrational parts of our brains. In other words, that which can fight viruses can also fight biases. We are really excited to have partnered with Bill and Melinda Gates on this video - and to help them get the word out about their annual letter about important global challenges.

[02:47] As you might expect, this year it's mostly about the ongoing COVID-19 pandemic. They point out that everyone in the world is connected by a microscopic network of germs—so, like it or not, we’re all in this together. In addition to discussing how we can mitigate some of the worst fallout from this pandemic - through equitable vaccine distribution and a huge investment in getting stalled educational opportunities back on track - they also dig into how we can prevent the next pandemic. That includes a global alert system that can quickly spot new threats, mega diagnostic platforms that can test 20% of the world’s population every week, and improved monoclonal antibody treatments and vaccines that can be rapidly deployed around the world.

[03:21] Check it out at GatesLetter.com or by clicking the link in the description. Thanks!

LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
Pandemic: A disease outbreak that spreads across the world.
Exponential Growth: Growth that occurs when a value increases in proportion to its current value.
Exponential Growth Prediction Bias: An error in prediction that occurs when people underestimate how quickly numbers rise in an exponential system. 
Optimism Bias: An error that occurs when people underestimate their personal likelihood of experiencing a negative event. 
Hyperbolic Time Discounting Bias: An error that occurs when people choose smaller rewards in the present over larger awards in the future.
Reactance Psychology: An unpleasant motivational state that occurs when a freedom is threatened with elimination. 
Individualism: The principle of being independent and self-reliant.
Collectivism: The principle of giving a group priority over each individual in it.
Disinformation: Deliberately false information designed to deceive.
