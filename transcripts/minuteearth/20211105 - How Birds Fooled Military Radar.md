# How Birds Fooled Military Radar

[00:00] Hi, this is Julián from MinuteEarth. Early in the Cold War, US military radar scanning for Soviet bombers detected that something had entered North American airspace. The US and Canada started prepping for action...although we don’t exactly know what action since all these files are confidential. But we do know that before any really drastic measures were taken, military intelligence discovered that what they assumed was a Soviet bomber was actually a flock of Canada geese. And this wasn’t just a fluke flock of fowl; since radar debuted in 1935, people using the technology to identify incoming aircraft and bad weather have regularly been fooled by birds. But we've since gotten really good at erasing birds from radar - which has, weirdly, ended up helping birds.

[00:42] That Soviet-spotting radar, like all radar, would send out radio waves and then “listen” for what bounced back off stuff in the sky. But it was only equipped to tell us that something was up there - it couldn't tell us anything about that thing. Today, we’ve improved radar technology to provide us with way more info, and we have computers capable of analyzing that data in real-time to give us a much clearer idea of what’s there. For example, we upgraded radar to detect shifts in wave frequencies, which gave us the ability to measure the speed of objects we were picking up in the sky - just like the radar that police use to catch speeders. This made it easy to distinguish birds from planes that fly hundreds of km/h. And to improve weather forecasting, we switched from single-polarization radars to dual-polarization radars, which transmit both horizontally and vertically-oriented waves at once. This gives us more data to evaluate the consistency in size and shape of a mass of things in the sky.

[01:36] A mass of nearly identically-sized and shaped things, like rain or snow, will return horizontally and vertically-aligned waves that are very similar in size and closely aligned with each other. But the less uniform things are, like hail or even more so, a flock of birds flapping their wings, the more the returned waves differ from each other. Computer algorithms can then filter out anything that's not uniform enough to be weather and get rid of it. But one scientist’s trash is another scientist’s treasure. Bird researchers realized they could use publicly available weather radar but filter it in reverse to get unprecedented information on birds. For years, conservationists couldn’t figure out where hundreds of thousands of threatened Canadian swallows were spending their time in-between breeding seasons.

[02:21] Even in perfectly clear skies, they can’t easily track swallows across the country...and it’s hard to preserve a species’ habitat if you don’t know where that habitat is. But with new-fangled radar and filtering techniques, conservationists can track the swallows along much of their migratory journey even through fowl weather and multiple rest stops. All they have to do is look for the beautiful, concentric blooms swallows create every time they leave their roosts, and continue to follow their radar signature. So, thanks to modern radar, tracking swallows is no longer a wild goose - or swallow - chase.

[02:56] This video’s sponsor, Nature Canada, has used these same radar techniques to identify potential roosting sites for threatened swallows around the lower Great Lakes in Ontario. Pinpointing the sites can still be difficult but where good radar data is available, Nature Canada's scientists and community partners can much more quickly start the fieldwork of confirming the roosting sites, counting the swallow species there, and determining the best ways to protect them. Nature Canada has been helping to save birds and other wildlife for over 80 years through conservation, education, and policy. LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
Radar aeroecology - the use of radar to study airborne life, like birds, bats, and bugs
Radar ornithology - the use of radar to study birds, especially bird migration
Dual-polarization radar - radar that transmits and receives waves in both a horizontal and vertical orientation, giving a better idea of a target’s size, shape, and variety.
Doppler effect - a change in a wave’s frequency in relation to whatever is observing it, such as a radar, but also your ears (it’s the reason a sound like an ambulance siren changes as it gets closer or further away from you).
