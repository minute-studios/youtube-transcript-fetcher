# The Mystery Of Asparagus Pee

[00:00] Asparagus is a green, white, or purple spike of a vegetable that heralds spring, goes well with eggs...and makes your pee smell really weird. Or, maybe it doesn't. About 4 out of 10 of people don’t seem to experience stinky asparagus pee, either because they don’t make it, or they can't smell it—or both. Great minds have pondered this smelly mystery since at least 1731, when a Scottish physician noticed that eating asparagus gave urine a definite funk. But, nearly 300 years later, we still don’t totally understand this stink partly because it isn't a particularly high-priority research area, and partly because despite centuries of scientific and technological progress, the gold standard for this research field still seems to involve sniffing pee.

[00:44] Pee sniffing has helped scientists figure out the basics. In a 2010 study, researchers collected pee from people both after they ate asparagus, and after they ate bread, then invited the urinators back to sniff everyone’s creations. Some people declined to return, perhaps because of what the researchers called an “unanticipated aversion to urine." Of the participants that did agree to sniff, a few couldn’t distinguish the samples of asparagus-pee from their bread-pee counterparts—which suggests that some people just can’t smell asparagus funk. We don’t know exactly why yet, but variation in a set of olfactory receptor genes may be what separates the asparagus-pee smellers from the non-smellers.

[01:19] What’s more, a few people in this study produced asparagus and bread pee samples that were indistinguishable to everyone, suggesting some people don’t make stinky asparagus pee. But here’s where pee sniffing falls short, because it’s also possible that something even stinkier in those samples masked the asparagus odor. To figure out what’s causing the stink, researchers turned to scientific sniffing, and found five sulfur-containing compounds that were up to thousands of times more abundant in the air above the stinky pee than above the non-stinky pee. These may be produced when an asparagus-specific molecule called "asparagussic" acid, or is it "asparagoosic"?

But that doesn’t explain why some people get stinky pee, and others don’t; perhaps stinky pee-ers — or their gut microbes — metabolize that molecule differently than their less funky counterparts. Maybe in the next 300 years, scientists will solve this Pee-Culiar mystery. But until then, there’s lots of asparagus to be eaten, and pee to be sniffed. It’s for science, people.
