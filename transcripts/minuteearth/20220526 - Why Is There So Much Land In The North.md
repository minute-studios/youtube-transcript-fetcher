# Why Is There So Much Land In The North?

[00:00] Yes, this map of the Earth IS upside down. We're showing it to you that way to jolt your brain into noticing something curious about our planet: the land is really unevenly distributed. More than 2/3 of it is in the … wait, no…ah, that’s better. More than 2/3 of the land on Earth is in the north. Welcome to LopsidedEarth. Over the years, people have come up with all sorts of hypotheses to explain this uneven distribution. Some thought that the rotation of earth was somehow pushing the continents upward, sort of like what happens to a smoothie in a blender. Others believed that, as the Earth cooled, it deflated into a saggy upside-down pyramid, and the oceans rushed to fill in the faces … which left land exposed on all the corners, most of which were in the north. And at least for a while, still others were sure that this uneven distribution wasn’t actually true; they thought there must be a massive-but-somehow-hidden southern continent that balanced out all that land in the North. But they were wrong - not just the invisible continent people, but everyone with clever theories about the northern-ness of the planet’s land. The actual reason there’s so much land in the northern hemisphere is that we’re basically looking at a random screenshot from a very long, very slow geological movie.

[01:03] In fact, it's just as likely that another screenshot would show most of the land in the south - or the east, or the west. That’s because the Earth’s land is constantly getting redistributed. The uneven heating and cooling of molten rock in the mantle creates convection currents that drag around the tectonic plates in a route that’s, well, all over the map. So if you paused the Earth movie at pangea 250 million years ago, 70% of the land would have been in the South. Around then, though, a combination of lots of extra heat bubbling up in the south and a downwelling of slightly cooler molten rock in the north started pulling most of the continental plates slowly toward the North-heavy arrangement we’re so familiar with.

[01:37] That particular arrangement may have actually been a lucky break for us as a species, since the interaction of tectonic plates created the unique features of the Great Rift Valley where humans first evolved; perhaps we wouldn’t even exist to be able to ponder this lopsidedness if the continents never took their current shape. But this configuration won’t last; the movie will go on. One recent model projects that, over the next 200 million years or so, the continental plates will once again smoosh together to form a supercontinent - this time one that’s shaped sort of like an overweight chicken - and is roughly half above and half below the equator - at least for the moment.

## Description

Most of Earth’s land is currently in the northern hemisphere because we happen to exist in a time where uneven heating in the mantle has pushed many continental plates northward.

LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
- Continental drift: An early theory of land movement that posited that the continents floated on the ocean bed. 
- Tetrahedral hypothesis: An early theory that attempted to explain the arrangement of Earth’s continents and oceans based on the geometry of a tetrahedron.
- Plate tectonics: The generally accepted theory that the Earth’s surface is made up of a series of tectonic plates that slowly move around on top of the mantle.
- Pangea: The most recent supercontinent to form on Earth, during the late Paleozoic era. 
- Great Rift Valley: A series of contiguous geographic trenches between two tectonic plates where humans first evolved.
- Aurica: A potential future supercontinent predicted to form in about 200 million years.
