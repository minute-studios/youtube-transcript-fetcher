# Why The Weather Is Worse At The Mall

[00:00] A surprisingly large number of freak hailstorms wreak havoc on shopping malls. And that’s not a coincidence. Welcome to MinuteEarth. Big malls - and their even bigger parking lots - are basically just giant slabs of concrete and asphalt that absorb the sun’s energy and radiate that heat back out, which can raise the temperature at a mega-mall by several degrees. That warm air rises, pushing up the top-most layer of the clouds, known as the “tropopause.” Meanwhile, the trees and grass that often border malls create shade and, through evaporative cooling, keep the surface temperature there cool, so the surrounding tropopause stays low and traps the rising air in a narrow column. As the column of air gets taller, the moisture in the clouds starts to condense, forming water droplets. At the same time, the volume of that column increases, creating a low-pressure vacuum effect that pulls air molecules towards the center, causing wind. In other words, it creates the perfect conditions for a storm to form. And the hotter the temperature on the ground, the quicker and higher the storm cloud rises into the frigid air above, increasing the likelihood that the water in it freezes into ice balls. The result: concentrated, destructive hailstorms focused directly over shopping malls.

[01:08] It’s not just malls, of course; any big difference in neighboring surface temperatures can trigger extreme weather. Mega-cities - known as heat islands - get up to 50% more rain than the tree-lined ‘burbs. And sun-baked farm fields cut into the forest are more likely to experience thunderstorms and tornadoes than the forests themselves. Luckily, through simple actions like planting trees in parking lots and sidewalks and farm fields for shade and evaporative cooling - or even painting some of those areas white so they don’t soak up as much heat - we can keep surface temperatures more uniform and limit destructive conditions from forming in the first place. Otherwise, there will be hail to pay.

LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
Evapotranspiration: the return of water to the atmosphere from the ground surface.
Troposphere: The lowest region of the Earth’s atmosphere, extending usually to around 10 km above the Earth’s surface.
Tropopause: The top-most boundary of the troposphere. 
Low Pressure System: An area of relatively low pressure that draws air into it, causing converging winds.
Micro-climate: The climate of a very small or restricted area.
Heat Island: An urban area that has higher average temperatures than its surrounding due to the heat absorption of materials like concrete and asphalt.
