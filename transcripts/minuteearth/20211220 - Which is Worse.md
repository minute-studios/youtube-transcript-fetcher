# Which is Worse?

[00:00] Assuming you’re looking to get back up and around quickly, you should hope for the broken bone. Bones are much more active and better equipped to rebuild themselves than ligaments are, helping fractures heal surprisingly quickly. Welcome to MinuteEarth. It’s easy to think of bones as lifeless, but the bones inside us are actually really lively. To stay strong, bones keep rebuilding themselves throughout much of your life. This process - called “remodelling” - replaces an un-bone-lieveable 20% of your skeleton every single year.

[00:36] On top of that, your bones have other jobs too; they’re also responsible for making new blood cells, and storing fat and minerals for use throughout your body. All this work requires lots of resources to provide energy and nutrients which your constantly-flowing blood carries to nearly every millimeter of your skeleton. So when a bone breaks, blood is right there; it immediately floods the fracture with the resources the bone needs to rebuild itself. With that bone-anza of supplies at hand, most fractures can heal in about six weeks.

[01:06] On the other hand, the bundles of connective tissue called “ligaments'' have basically one job - holding you together - and don’t remodel over time, so they don’t need many resources. Instead of a bone-ified blood supply, ligaments rely on energy and nutrients that diffuse secondhand from nearby tissues. So when a ligament gets damaged - what we call a “sprain” - the resources it needs to repair itself arrive in a trickle, rather than a flood. That’s why sprains take way longer than fractures to heal - at least ten weeks.

[01:37] And certain ligaments - like the ACL in your knee - are far enough from a blood supply that they may never heal on their own. This rule of thumb - a better blood supply equals faster healing - holds true across most of your body, from super-slow-healing cartilage, which has even less access to blood than ligaments, to muscles, which are basically full of blood and, as a result, can heal in days. Weirdly though, the fastest-healing tissue in your body - the cornea - has no direct blood supply; after all, if your corneas, the windows at the front of your eyes, were full of blood, it would leave you seeing red.
