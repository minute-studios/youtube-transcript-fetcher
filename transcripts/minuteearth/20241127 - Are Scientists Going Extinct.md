# Are Scientists Going Extinct?

[00:00] Artificial intelligence programs can write news stories, code apps, and handle customer service; they can do all these things well enough that, in some cases, companies are replacing the humans doing these jobs with AI. Which makes me—a nerd who trained, for years, to become a scientist—wonder whether AI is capable of doing that job too…and if so, what outsourcing all future scientific discovery to a bunch of machines might mean for science, and for the world.

[00:30] Oh yeah – hi, I’m Kate and this is MinuteEarth. A scientist’s job involves all sorts of tasks, like gathering information – through data collection or just observing stuff; noticing patterns or oddities; generating potential explanations; designing experiments, doing those experiments; and documenting the findings – not to mention less-science-y seeming tasks, like supervising students and writing grants. In other words, science requires bringing together two pretty different sides of humanity: logic and creativity.

[01:02] Which seems like a tall order for AI to replicate. Lots of AI systems ARE great at logic-based tasks. They're programmed with tons of training data related to solving a particular problem. Then, when presented with a new instance of that problem, they run through all the possible solutions, and pick out the one that, based on the training data, is most likely to be successful. Scientists have been using these types of programs for a while to tackle the logic-based parts of their job, like combing through massive datasets to find exoplanets, and identifying the best conditions for growing carbon nanotubes.

[01:35] But nowadays, there’s also AI built for creativity. These programs are trained on a bunch of very general data and, when they’re presented with a prompt, they come up with an output by …well, we don’t know exactly how. Which is actually somewhat similar to human creativity; based on what we’ve experienced, we can—through processes that we don’t fully understand—invent entirely new things. Scientists are beginning to put these programs to work in creative parts of their job, like generating hypotheses about the properties of unknown substances.

[02:07] Since the systems that excel in logical tasks aren’t good at the creative ones, and vice versa, AI hasn’t been a legit threat to scientists’ entire jobs…phew! However, researchers recently developed a few “AI scientists” that can carry out the entire process of scientific discovery, from gathering info to publishing the results. So far, these AI proto-scientists have been constrained to researching machine learning itself, and what they’ve come up with are basically tweaks that speed up or improve existing methodology, rather than anything truly mind-blowing. That’s because, although AI is REALLY good at logic-based stuff, it’s unclear whether its capacity for real creativity is anywhere near that of humans’—in other words, whether AI can develop new ideas that change how we think about the world, like humans’ greatest scientific discoveries have. So we still don’t know whether AI could legitimately replace the work human scientists do, rather than just supplement it.

[03:06] Even if we could eventually outsource science entirely to AI, should we? In some ways, automating scientific discovery is really exciting; like, it could drastically speed up advancement in certain fields, like the development of treatments for underfunded diseases...just think of all the potential lives we could improve, or even save! But consider those very same capabilities put to more nefarious purposes; like, it only took a few hours for one AI program to invent 40,000 new possibly-deadly molecules. Can we trust AI—which we don’t even fully understand—to wield science wisely…or at least as wisely as humans? What’s more, science includes AI research and development, so completely automating science would put AI in charge of researching and designing itself, which has the potential to go really wrong. And we're not even getting into all sorts of other challenges associated with AI, like all the energy it takes to train and run these systems, or biases baked into them via the training data. There are just so many unknowns when it comes to science and AI…figuring out what might happen down the road—and what to do about it now—is a job that I’m glad we have a lot of very smart human scientists around to do.
