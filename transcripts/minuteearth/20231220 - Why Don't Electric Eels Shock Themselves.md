# Why Don't Electric Eels Shock Themselves?

[00:00] Hi, I’m Leo. I usually spend my time over on the Brazilian version of MinuteEarth –MinutoDaTerra– but today I’ve come over here to tell you about my obsession with one particular animal from my homeland –the electric eel– or, as we call it in Portuguese, “Poraque” meaning the one who puts you to sleep. An electric eel can deliver a shock of as much as 860 volts, which is about seven times higher than the voltage of the average US power outlet. That’s enough to paralyze other animals, or even kill them outright. Here in Brazil, we all know how dangerous these creatures are. But I could never figure out one thing – why doesn’t an electric eel’s shock hurt the eel itself?” To get a handle on this eel-ectrifying question, you first have to understand how eels generate and use electric currents, and how these currents can cause damage in an animal’s body. Being able to produce electric shocks sounds… well… shocking, but all animals are capable of producing electricity – even you. Every time an animal’s nerve or muscle cell fires, it generates an itty-bitty amount of electricity; these tiny jolts do all sorts of important jobs, from regulating an animal’s heartbeat to making it’s body move. From these cells, which produce electricity as a means for accomplishing other jobs, eels evolved cells called “electrocytes,” whose ONLY function is to generate electricity. Eighty percent of an eel’s body is made up of electrocytes, stacked up like cells in a battery pack. An eel IS actually a lot like a battery pack – but instead of having 9 volts, it has several hundred! And instead of passing through a wire, like the current generated by a battery, the current generated by an EEL passes through the WATER the eel lives in as well as the bodies of any animals that happen to be IN that water.

[01:42] And that brings us to how the current affects other animals. All sorts of an animal’s biological functions are regulated via its own internal electrical circuitry. The current emitted by an eel messes with those functions; for instance, by triggering involuntary muscle contractions that can paralyze the critter, allowing the eel to either eat it or escape from it. The eel's jolts are effective weapons – so how do they avoid shocking themselves and other eels around them? The answer is that they probably DO shock themselves – but only a little bit. If you imagine the eel’s electric organs as a battery, then there are two paths for current to travel from one side to the other: through the water, or through the eel. Current will travel along both paths, but since the body of the eel has much higher electrical resistance than the water, only a little bit of the current will travel through it; most of the current will go out into, well, the current. What’s more, the eel has another trick for limiting damage to its internal electrical system. Most of an eel’s vital organs are smooshed together at the front of its body – outside the direct path of the current – and surrounded by a thick layer of fat that provides extra insulation. So, for electric eels, staving off a shock to themselves seems to come down to creating the path of MOST resistance.
