# We Have No Idea Why

[00:00] Fireflies are unusual animals, but not because their butts light up; light-up body parts are actually really common in the animal world. Instead, what makes fireflies so weird is that they don’t live in the ocean. I’m Cameron, and this is MinuteEarth. To us humans, light-up creatures seem really rare. That's because only a handful of the animals we regularly see - those on land, in the air, and in lakes and rivers - are bioluminescent - which means they make their own light: there are fireflies, some beetles, glow worms, millipedes, and a single species of freshwater snail. But the vast majority of animals in the ocean – around 75 percent of them – make light in one way or another, which means lighting up in the ocean is the rule, not an exception. We don’t really know why bioluminescence is so common in the ocean but so scarce everywhere else. Perhaps one reason for this is darkness.

[00:57] Much of the ocean is dark or dim, so lighting up there might be a good way to startle potential predators. It’s also useful for connecting with mates, or even to lure prey to a toothy doom. But there are non-ocean animals that live in places where it’s dark: The poles, for example, where it’s dark for months at a time, or in caves, where it’s dark pretty much all the time. Also, let’s not forget the animals that are only active at night. In all cases, these animals have the same needs as ocean dwellers, and could conceivably benefit from light up parts as well.

[01:30] So darkness alone doesn't seem to explain why so few animals outside the ocean light up. Perhaps non ocean animals just haven’t had the time to evolve bioluminescence. Ocean animals did have a ~200 million year head start on animals that live on land and in freshwater, so they have had tons of extra time to develop light-making mechanisms, and maybe in 200 million more years non-ocean animals will have caught up. But ray-finned fishes have independently evolved light-making machinery at least 27 times in the last 150 million years, which suggests that bioluminescence actually evolves pretty quickly. So non-sea creatures should have had plenty of time to evolve light-up parts too.

[02:14] So maybe it’s because of obstacles, or a lack thereof. Non-ocean environments, like forests, are too full of light-blocking obstructions that might make lighting up there not as useful as it is in the open ocean. Or maybe bioluminescence is just easier in water. Light-making chemicals might build up to toxic levels if an animal can’t process them fast enough, and water might be great at washing those chemicals away before they become deadly. But neither of those ideas can explain why freshwater lakes – especially ones like Lake Baikal, which are super deep and dark - have no bioluminescent species. Maybe salt in seawater plays a role in making light, but experiments haven’t supported that idea…or really any of the others, for that matter.

[03:02] Which… is unsatisfying, but I think you get the point – we are still trying to figure out why bioluminescence is only a bright idea in the ocean - for now, we’re still in the dark. science stories, including a holographic sticker sheet with awesome bioluminescent animals! LEARN MORE

To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
- Bioluminescence: the biochemical emission of light by living organisms such as fireflies and deep-sea fishes.
- Biofluorescence: The emission of previously absorbed light by fluorescent proteins in a living organism.
- Luciferin: an organic substance, present in luminescent organisms such as fireflies, that produces light when oxidized by the action of the enzyme luciferase.
