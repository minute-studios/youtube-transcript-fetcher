# Why There Are No King Bees

[00:00] When a male honey bee mates with a queen, his abdomen bursts open. This is quite a buzzkill for him, but it's not a problem for the queen, because she goes on to mate with other enthusiastic party poppers to gather genes for her hive. But I can't help but wonder: What if the queen's first mate didn't die? Let's imagine he has an abdomen of steel or something. Could he, alongside the queen, be a king bee? Hi, I’m Cameron, and this is MinuteEarth. The queen bee isn't a queen in the sense that she makes rules – she's a queen in the sense that she lays all the eggs, which makes her the doted-upon mother of all of the bees in the hive.

[00:34] And even though we are all familiar with the lone queen bee – many hive-dwelling species, like termites, also have kings who serve as the father of the entire hive, so it's not like there is no precedent for a king bee. But even with abs of steel, our hypothetical king is a non-starter, because bee genetics are bee-zarre. It starts out a lot like how humans – or termites, for that matter – pass on their genes; female bees put half of their complement of chromosomes in each egg. A male’s sperm contributes an equal number of chromosomes, and that fertilized egg – with a full set of chromosomes – becomes a baby.

[01:08] But here’s where things get weird – honey bee eggs can become baby bees even without a male’s chromosomal contribution. The difference is that fertilized eggs – with two sets of chromosomes, which is a full complement of bee genes – develop into female bees, any of which could become a new queen under the right conditions, and unfertilized eggs – with only one set of chromosomes– become male bees. Male bees, in other words, ONLY get genes from their mother. This weird genetic quirk means that a male bee can only have daughters – not sons.

[01:38] Of course, biological sex is complicated and it’s possible for honey bee eggs with two sets of chromosomes to be male, but they end up sterile – and the workers murder them as babies anyway. But even if we allow the king to only father daughters and potential new queens, a single male bee still couldn't father a hive's worth of them. Because he passes along all of his genes to each daughter as opposed to half of them like the queen does, every daughter of his is super-genetically-related, which, if they all happen to inherit the same deadly mutation from their father, could be super disastrous for a hive with a single father.

[02:10] This genetic diversity issue is likely one reason –aside from all the death– that the queen racks up a large, ahem, body count. But I want to take this hypothetical exercise a tiny bit farther; what if having a full complement of chromosomes made a bee male, and a half complement made a bee female? Keeping all else the same for the sake of our thought experiment – could we have a king bee then? Well, no. In this scenario, fertilized eggs would become males, and unfertilized eggs would develop into females. But this doesn’t improve things for our wanna-be king; here, males still only father one sex –males– and now there is an additional problem.

[02:46] In this hypothetical situation, female bees can produce daughters – again, any of which could become another queen – with no male contribution at all. Granted, they will run into the same genetic variation problem that a lone king would create, but ultimately, the male is completely irrelevant to the future of the hive here. So no matter how far we buzz down this hypothetical trail, we just can’t make king bees work. A honeybee hive can only ever have a queen. And while she doesn’t wear a crown; she’s got a fabulous pair of mom genes.

To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
- Haplodiploidy: is a sex-determination system in which males develop from unfertilized eggs and are haploid, and females develop from fertilized eggs and are diploid.
- Sex determination in honeybees: In honeybees, the drones (males) are entirely derived from the queen, their mother. The diploid queen has 32 chromosomes and the haploid drones have 16 chromosomes. Drones produce sperm cells that contain their entire genome, so the sperm are all genetically identical except for mutations.
- Eusocial: showing an advanced level of social organization, in which a single female or caste produces offspring and nonreproductive individuals cooperate in caring for the young.
- Fertilize: cause (an egg, female animal, or plant) to develop a new individual by introducing male reproductive material.
- Genetics: the study of heredity and the variation of inherited characteristics.
- Queen Bee: the single reproductive female in a hive or colony of honeybees.
- Sterile: not able to produce children or young.
