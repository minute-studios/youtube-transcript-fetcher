# Bitcoin mining is a lot like reindeer mating 🪙💖🦌

[00:00] Bitcoin mining is a lot like reindeer mating. Ok, that analogy is a bit weird, but it’s won-deer-ful for explaining why Bitcoin mining – which is the processing transactions of the digital currency and creating new Bitcoin – uses such a ridiculous amount of energy. You see, male reindeer compete for mates, and the largest male with the largest antlers is most likely to win the fight and get the girl…girls, really, since males usually compete for up to 15 females at a time. Bitcoin-mining computers, too, compete. Every 10 minutes, they go head-to-head in a race to correctly guess the answer to a really complicated math equation. The most powerful computer, capable of making the most computations per second, is most likely to win, earning them the right to mate with all the–I mean, the right to confirm a group of Bitcoin transactions and get some Bitcoin of their own. So whether you're a male reindeer or a computer on the Bitcoin network, the more powerful you are, the more successful you're likely to be.

[00:54] But being powerful requires a lot of energy. Compared to smaller reindeer, the biggest males need to consume much more food to fuel their massive bodies and grow large antlers for the mating season. Similarly, compared to the most basic Bitcoin mining hardware, the most powerful Bitcoin-mining operations gobble up tens of thousands of times more energy. And that energy-use is constantly growing because just like the competition for dominance among male reindeer has led to the evolution of larger reindeer with larger antlers, competition is pushing Bitcoin miners to use computers that are ever-more powerful and energy-hungry. The result? An arms race resulting in ever-higher energy consumption.

[01:33] But that doesn't mean energy use will increase forever. If energy is hard to come by, reindeer – and their antlers – can't get all that big; that's why reindeer living in places where food is abundant and easy to find, grow the largest bodies and antlers. And if energy is hard to come by - or too expensive - Bitcoin miners must scale back their computer power, which is why places where energy is reliable and cheap attract the most energy-hungry Bitcoin miners. Over millions of years of evolution, reindeer as a species have basically gotten as beefy as they're likely to get - at least given the energy that they currently have available.

[02:06] But since Bitcoin is still early in its evolution, this exponential growth is likely to continue. Unless, that is, the value of Bitcoin stops increasing or how it’s mined fundamentally changes. Hopefully, it doesn’t take millions of years to rein it in.
