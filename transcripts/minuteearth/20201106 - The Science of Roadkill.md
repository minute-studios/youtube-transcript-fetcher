# The Science of Roadkill

[00:00] Hi this is David from MinuteEarth. If you wanted to design the perfect predator, you’d make it huge, impossibly strong, incredibly fast, and capable of freezing its prey as it approached. And we've actually built pretty much that very thing: a car. And while cars aren’t car-nivores, every day they mow down millions of animals - and that doesn’t even include insects. All this death, though, has an unexpected upside: the car-casses are actually a bone-anza for scientists. Not because scientists are morbid, but because they're resourceful. Like, say you wanted to survey hedgehogs. Normally, you'd have to tromp through well, hedges, night after night, maybe dragging along expensive equipment, just to find the elusive critters.

[00:34] And to gather information about individual hedgehogs, you’d have to use fancy traps, and get special permits. Or you could just find them flattened on the pavement, document where you found them, gather the specimens, and study them to your heart’s content. In fact, roadkill has actually helped us learn all sorts of stuff about our planet. Scientists in Southern Europe have found more smooshed snakes earlier and earlier in the Spring, suggesting that the rising temperatures caused by climate change are drawing them out of their burrows sooner. In the Everglades, a sudden lack of wrecked raccoons, squashed opossums, and battered bunnies indicated that something was amiss - and that something turned out to be invasive pythons nomming all the inhabitants of the swamp before they could even get to the road.

[01:11] And by studying the bowels of plowed owls, we found that pesticide-laden rats make up a large portion of the diet of many birds of prey - putting even non-squashed raptors at risk. But different animals don’t all get flattened with the same frequency, right? Doesn't this make it harder to draw scientific conclusions from roadkill? Owls for example don’t seem to notice cars, which makes them relatively common victims. Pronghorns, on the other hand, speed up when crossing the road, making them less likely to get hit, while bats detect and avoid traffic altogether. On the other end of the spectrum, armadillos tend to freeze when cars approach, so they're extra likely to get mowed down.

[01:43] And since scavengers like crows like to nom on flattened fauna, they often end up flattened too. But as long as scientists understand this variation, they can account for it in their analysis. OK, so this is all a little morbid and a lot depressing, but roadkill ecologists are simply following in the footsteps of other opportunistic researchers, like marine biologists who search through fishing boat nets to find new deep sea dwellers. Or engineers who study storm-damaged structures to identify better building materials. Or the archeologists who excavate mass graves to reveal the mysteries of ancient civilizations.

[02:14] Or the astronomers who study the remains of star explosions to understand the laws that govern our universe. They’re simply part of the grand tradition of scientists who turn car-nage into knowledge. Ahh, I never get tire-d of a good pun. LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
Roadkill: The remains of animals killed by motor vehicles.
Road ecology: The study of how roads impact the surrounding environment and its inhabitants.
Invasive species: Non-native animals that outcompete local animals for resources.
Fieldwork: The collection of data outside of a laboratory setting.
Confounder: A variable (like animal reaction to oncoming cars) that influences the outcome when comparing a dependent variable (like number of animals) to the outcome (number of roadkill carcasses of said animal).
