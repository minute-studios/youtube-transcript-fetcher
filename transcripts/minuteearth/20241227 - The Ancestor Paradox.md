# The Ancestor Paradox

[00:00] This video is sponsored by MyHeritage. More about them at the end of the episode. Because you have two parents, and each of those parents has two parents, and so on, your ancestors – parents, grandparents, and great-grandparents and so on – should add up pretty fast. In fact, by the time you get just 37 generations back, you'd have 137 billion ancestors just in that 37th generation alone. That’s more than the 117 billion or so people that have ever lived on Earth – which is impossible! So you must have a lot fewer ancestors than you might think.

[00:31] Hi, I’m Cameron, and this is MinuteEarth. To make sense of this, let’s say for example, I dunno, that your parents are first cousins. I know, bear with me for a second… That would mean that two of your grandparents are siblings. Since siblings have the same parents, and therefore the same ancestry, a bunch of these ancestors are duplicates; we can really just count them once. Going back to our 37 generations thing, that’s more than 34 BILLION ancestors we would no longer be counting! That might sound like an extreme example, but more distant relationships, like fourth cousins, which we don’t even consider to be that closely related – still hollow out your pyramid. And our family histories are full of lots and lots of inbreeding. That might sound pretty gross to you and me, but mating with relatives hasn't always been frowned upon – heck, Albert Einstein married his first cousin. And certain royal families actively encouraged inbreeding.

[01:23] But the biggest reason for all this inbreeding is that, throughout most of human history, we lived in small groups with limited babymaking options, which led to… a lot of trees that just don’t fork very much. But wait – so far, we’ve been defining your ancestors as everyone you are descended from; if instead, we think about an ancestor as someone that actually passed genes onto you, the number is much, MUCH smaller. I know that sounds paradoxical – I mean, you had to get your genes from somewhere, so of course they came from your ancestors. But they didn’t come from ALL of your ancestors.

[01:55] Imagine that instead of passing along their genes, people passed along different kinds of candy. To inherit your genome, you close your eyes and draw an equal number of candies from your mom’s bowl and your dad’s bowl – each of which contain a mix of everything they each got from their ancestors. There are going to be a lot of candies that you don’t get – these represent genes that you don’t inherit. This is obviously an oversimplification, but I hope you get the idea. You can see how very quickly, you might by chance receive fewer or no genes from some ancestors because you received extra from another. Over time, this process means that most of your distant ancestors didn’t pass on any DNA to you.

[02:31] If you do the math – which is complicated – you have only a 50% chance of having inherited any DNA at all from a randomly-selected ancestor ten generations back, and a three percent chance of inheriting anything from an ancestor fifteen generations back. The majority of your ancestors farther back than that almost certainly didn't pass along any DNA to you. The DNA you did inherit came from only a very small slice of the pyramid of all of your supposed ancestors. So your family tree might be smaller than you think, and your gene pool is even smaller.

LEARN MORE
**************
To learn more about this topic, start your googling with these keywords: 
- Genealogy: a line of descent traced continuously from an ancestor.
- Genetic ancestor: a person from whom an individual is biologically descended
- Genetic ancestry: the genetic origin of a person's population, and the specific lines of descent through which they inherited DNA from their ancestors.
- Pedigree: the recorded ancestry, especially upper-class ancestry, of a person or family.
- Pedigree collapse: a term used in genealogy to describe when the number of distinct ancestors in a family tree is smaller than it would be otherwise.
