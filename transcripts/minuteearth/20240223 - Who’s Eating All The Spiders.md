# Who’s Eating All The Spiders?

[00:00] So there’s a lot of folk wisdom out there about humans and animals. Some of it is true – I mean dogs can detect people’s cancer and koalas do have fingerprints that are pretty much identical to our own – but others, not so much. For example, they say that the average human swallows around 3 spiders every year. And, I mean… no. Spiders can sense human vibrations and carbon dioxide and generally stay far… –Yes, Arcadi? Yeah, oh, what about Spiders Georg? What the heck is Spiders Georg?

[00:32] Hi! I’m Arcadi, and this is MinuteEarth. This is a story about statistics and old Tumblr memes and about how easy it is for numbers to distort how we understand the world around us... But I'm getting ahead of myself. Let’s say that almost everyone on Earth swallows a total of zero spiders per year. Wait – Cameron, what is this? Did you eat a spider? Ok, whatever, not gonna judge. SO, since the vast majority of people don’t eat any spiders, between all of us we should average around 0 spiders eaten per year – not three. But here’s where things get interesting: meet Spiders Georg. We don’t really know what his deal is, but according to old-school Tumblr, he lives in a cave and, every day, Georg gorges on millions of spiders. If we average Georg’s yearly spider intake in with the rest of the world’s – that is, the total number of spiders eaten divided by the total number of people – we indeed find that on average, each person on Earth DOES consume three spiders per year. Ok – and here's the part where I take an internet joke way too seriously – but you know, using the number we've just calculated – which nerds like me refer to as the MEAN – to mean "average" can be misleading – I mean, almost everyone on Earth eats NO spiders at all!

[01:50] Ok, so here’s what’s going on: the “mean” tends to be more useful in datasets where the data mostly falls around a particular peak, like this chart of men’s heights. Here, the peak is right around 5’10”. Some people are way taller or way shorter than that, and there’s a lot of people, like me, who are around that average height. Here, the “mean” does a pretty good job describing a typical height for a guy to be. But data doesn't always follow that nice one-peaked distribution. And when that's the case, means have less meaning. Like, this is the amount of cars that use a certain road during certain times of the day. We’ve got the rush hours at 7 AM and 5 PM and then bits of relative calm, like in the middle of the night. If we wanted to find the “mean” driving hour, we’d get that 2 PM is the average hour to be driving at, but, I mean, no: there’s only a handful of people driving at 2 PM – it’s an atypical time to be driving at.

[02:45] Or, let’s look at the distribution of income in the US – that is, how much money people earn in a year. We COULD say that the mean income for someone in the US is around $59k a year, but, once we take a look at the full data, we can see… Well, what we already knew: most people earn less than that, and there’s just a few that earn more. But the ones who earn more, earn waaaaayyy more, which ends up driving the mean way up, just like Georg did to our spider eating dataset. As a result, economists generally find it more useful to focus on the middle point in the dataset – called the median: half of Americans earn MORE than $40k, and the other half earns less than that. That’s likely more representative of the average Joe than of the Money Georgs of the world.

[03:32] If we did the same for our spider-eating dataset, we'd come up with a median of, uh… zero spiders! Allright, that feels more useful. Or we could find the most common amount of spiders to eat – the mode of this set. That’s also zero spiders. We could remove the highest and lowest values from the dataset before averaging it… There’s so many techniques we could use! And they would show that – even if Spiders Georg existed – almost everyone has a spider-free diet. In other words, using the “mean” to mean “average” – isn’t always meaningful.
