# Why Do Weeping Willows Weep?

[00:00] Most trees reach for the sun. Then, there’s the weeping willow. Sure, its trunk grows upwards, but on the whole, these trees droop down, basically growing toward the ground. What – or who – are they weeping for? Hi, I’m Kate and this is MinuteEarth. First, not all willows weep; hundreds of willow species exist worldwide, and most of them have a more standard upward-reaching shape. Also, willows aren’t the only trees that weep; lots of trees – like the poplar, hemlock, maple, acacia, cherry, and peach – also have weepy varieties.

[00:34] The droopy demeanor of weepy trees – and, actually the overall shape of all trees – stems from the same cause: hormones. Hormones – which are directed by a tree’s genes – help a tree recognize which way is up, establish the angles of its branches, and determine how long and thick those branches get. But occasionally, a tree will have a genetic mutation that makes its hormones act in weird ways. Some of these mutations make a tree grow more upright, and others cause it to droop down toward the ground – a form we, poetically, call “weeping”.

[01:08] But let’s go one level deeper. I mean, mutations happen all the time, but most die out…why has weeping carried on in all sorts of different trees? It’s possible that in certain situations, droopy branches benefit trees – maybe by maximizing the light that reaches a tree’s leaves, or minimizing moisture loss – enabling weepers to survive and pass on their mutation. But I dug pretty deep into the research out there, and there’s just no evidence of these kinds of benefits. Here’s the thing: most weeping trees out there didn’t get there on their own.

[01:41] Take the weeping hemlock, a tree for which we have a remarkably complete history. Most hemlocks have that standard, upright shape, but in the 1850’s, a farmer in New York discovered a single weeping one. People became obsessed with this tree’s weird shape, and they took lots of small pieces from it – pieces that they used to grow their own weeping hemlocks. Weeping beech shares a similar story; every weeping beech in America is said to be descended from a single shoot imported from Belgium in 1847. And the weeping willow, too, which likely originated as a rare variant of an upright willow species in China; these unusual willows were cultivated widely in Asia, and were eventually traded via the Silk Road to Europe and beyond.

[02:23] We humans are the force that has spread weeping trees far and wide, because we find beauty in them. And meaning too; in different cultures, weeping trees symbolize resilience, femininity, hope, and grief. In other words, weeping trees have followed a path to success that a lot of other plants and animals have; they became common because we humans wanted them around. Willows, it seems, weep for us.
